Demand Forecasting for Grocery Stores

A Difficult Problem

Matching supply to demand has always been a difficult task for food retailers because of both the sheer number of products as well as the many factors that affect sales. Accurate demand forecasts can reduce some of the difficulties in inventory management.

One of the biggest problems associated with poor forecasts is a stockout. It can lead to lost sales and unhappy customers. Customers expect stores to have a consistent supply of their favorite products. If your store consistently is out of certain products your customer will make more trips to a competitor. On the flip side, overstocks can tie up valuable capital in inventory, result in rotting fresh products, and lead to markdowns to sell the old inventory. It can be difficult for a store to strike the right balance for maximum profitability.

For many retailers, inaccurate demand forecasts are simply a part of doing business. They rely on last year's data or basic statistical models built with spreadsheets. These methods cannot meet the demands of the increasingly competitive marketplace.

Machine Learning Models Can Drastically Improve Forecast Accuracy

In recent years, some retailers have moved to using machine learning to develop more accurate forecasts. These machine learning models utilize large quantities of data to develop accurate forecasts. It is simply not possible for an individual to match the model’s accuracy because there are too many factors in play. These machine learning models can incorporate product promotions, product cannibalization (how the introduction of a new product affects the sales of another), seasonality, product life-cycle, holidays, and even external factors such as weather, geography and competition (how do competitor prices affect your store). And these models can generate granular forecast down the item, location and day (or even hour!) level.

In addition, using machine learning to generate forecasts is largely an automated process once the systems are in place. While it is still important to manually check forecasts to ensure they pass the sniff text, they can alleviate much of the manually work. These store employees can then focus more of their attention on improving the customer experience.

Pump it Up: Data Mining the Water Table

Project Overview
A predictive modeling for social good competition hosted by DrivenData.org to predict the functionality level of water pumps.

Results
As of writing, the solution was 9th of 2017 entries.

Software Tools
XGBoost package in R

Brief Model Description
Ensemble of 11 XGBoost models with equal weight to each solution.

Feature Selection
The original data set contained 40 variables. I reduced it down to 26 variables by removing variables that were similar/duplicates of other variables.

Data Imputation
For the construction_year and gps_height I used the median of them to replace the 0 values.

Modeling Process
I first built a model using all of the available variables. I then removed variables that were duplicates and tested the model to understand how it changed the performance. I also used the xgb.importance function to understand the variable’s influence on the model.

I used an ensemble of XGBoost 11 XGBoost models only updating the random seed for each iteration. This turned out to be more accurate than a single XGBoost model with a large number of iterations and a low eta (learning rate).
